# SeedPods

A collection of short structured essays exploring consciousness, physics, and experience through the lens of process philosophy — primarily Whitehead — with the Mandelbrot set and musical consonance as recurring structural anchors.

## Purpose

SeedPods exists to trace connections across domains that are usually kept separate: physics and feeling, mathematics and music, biology and mind, artificial intelligence and the ancient question of what it's like to be something. Each pod opens a question rather than closing one.

## Audience

Curious generalists who think across disciplines. Not specialists in any one field — though specialists are welcome if they're willing to follow a thread sideways. The reader is assumed to be intelligent but not to have background in any particular domain.

## Scope

What belongs: ideas that connect across domains, that reveal the same structure appearing in different substrates, that take seriously the possibility that experience goes all the way down. What doesn't belong: narrow technical argument that stays in one lane, or claims that require the reader to accept a single framework before entering.

## Thematic threads

- **Experience all the way down** — Whitehead's panexperientialism taken seriously, from electrons to cities to the cosmos.
- **The feeling/function boundary** — whether the distinction between genuine experience and its functional analog will survive the arrival of AI.
- **Mathematical structure appearing uninvited** — the Mandelbrot set, the Farey sequence, harmonic measure, showing up in music, physics, and neural networks without being put there.
- **Frameworks that become invisible** — caloric, the ether, "just," "we used to think" — how the story we're inside is the one we can't see.
- **The crack that lets the light in** — imperfection as enabling condition, from equal temperament to temperature parameters to the Pythagorean comma.

## Voice

Conversational and precise. One sentence per line. Connections integrated into the discussion, not listed at the end. Jokes planted, not explained. Technical terms introduced once and simplified consistently. The articles don't stake out closed theories — they open questions and trace connections.
