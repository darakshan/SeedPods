## ScriptCraft

These notes are intended for addition to the Structure page under Layer 4: Script.  Learned in practice, session of 2026-03-11

The script is not an illustrated voiceover. It is a film. Start from what the camera can show and earn every word of narration.

**On POV**
The most powerful tool for conveying an interior is point of view. Don't tell the audience someone is conscious — put the audience inside their experience. They will feel it before they understand it.

**On sound**
Sound design can carry a turn that no narration could. In seedpod #22, the shift from muffled to clear — the moment someone finally addresses Rom directly — is the emotional climax. It costs nothing to write and everything to experience. Sound is the color of film. You don't always need to go from black and white to color — a shift in acoustic quality can do the same work more subtly.

**On time**
Resist title cards. Let the world of the film show time passing — through objects, through faces, through what has changed in a room. A mother's hair going from dark to white is worth ten years of title cards. Give the director the idea and trust them with the execution — a brief note in the script is enough.

**On voiceover**
Use it sparingly — only when the image alone genuinely cannot carry the idea. Two moments of voiceover in a three-minute film is probably the maximum. More than that and you are writing an essay, not a film.

**On the closing image**
The script should end on something the eye can hold, not something the ear just heard. Text on black is a last resort. A held image is better. The final frame should be able to stand alone as a still.

**On props and objects**
Objects do narrative work that dialogue cannot. A monitor growing more sophisticated over the years, a phone becoming a smartphone, a mother's reading glasses appearing and then thickening — these are not decoration. They are the story told sideways, which is often the most powerful way to tell it.

**General principle**
Show the inside by controlling what the audience sees and hears from the outside. The script's job is to engineer that experience — not to explain it.
