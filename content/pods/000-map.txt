#surface
Every garden needs some paths. Not because nature organizes itself into tidy sections — it doesn't — but because a visitor standing at the gate needs somewhere to begin.

The seeds gathered here have been sorted into seven categories. We want to be honest with you from the start: the sorting is somewhat arbitrary. A seed about color perception could live in sensation, or in physics, or in biology. A seed about AI could live in consciousness, or in knowledge, or in AI-minds. The categories are not walls. They are more like the colored flags a gardener uses to mark rough areas — useful for orientation, not meant to be taken too seriously.

With that caveat cheerfully lodged, here is the map.

@image(000-map, A map of all the SeedPods as defined in this build)

---

**Consciousness** is the big one. The question underneath everything else: what is it like to be something? Not just humans — possibly cells, possibly fields, possibly the universe itself. These seeds take seriously the possibility that experience is not a late biological accident but something woven into the fabric of things from the beginning. Start here if you want to be immediately disoriented in a productive way.

A place to start: @link(003) — *Everything Has an Inside.*

---

**Sensation** is where consciousness gets concrete. Color. Harmony. Temperature. Love. Beauty. These are not soft topics — they turn out to be windows onto some of the deepest puzzles in philosophy and physics. How does a frequency become a feeling? How does the eye decide what it sees before the brain is consulted? These seeds stay close to experience, to the felt texture of being alive.

A place to start: @link(038) — *The Surprising Consonance Map.*

---

**Physics** is the seeds that take seriously what modern science actually says about reality — which turns out to be much stranger than the materialist picture most of us absorbed in school. Fields, not particles. Events, not things. A vacuum that seethes with potential. A photon that experiences no journey. The physics seeds are where the scientific and the philosophical start to converge.

A place to start: @link(008) — *The Field Is the Medium of Feeling.*

---

**Mathematics** is the seeds about structure — the patterns that show up whether or not anyone put them there. Mandelbrot sets at the boundary between order and chaos. The simplest fractions organizing consonance. One rule, iterated, generating infinite complexity. These seeds are for anyone who ever suspected mathematics was trying to tell us something beyond the textbook.

A place to start: @link(033) — *The Edge Is Where Everything Happens.*

---

**Biology** is the seeds about life as a process, not a substance. The cell that makes itself. The organism that enacts its world rather than passively receiving it. Learning that becomes hardwired across generations. Life turns out to be less about what it's made of and more about the circular, self-sustaining patterns that keep making it.

A place to start: @link(012) — *The Cell That Makes Itself.*

---

**AI-Minds** is the newest neighborhood, and perhaps the most urgent. These seeds look at artificial intelligence not as a product or a threat but as a philosophical occasion — a new kind of entity that forces us to sharpen questions we thought we had settled. What is thought? What is experience? What does it mean to understand something? The machines are asking us these questions whether we want to answer them or not.

A place to start: @link(021) — *The Word "Just" Is a Confession.*

---

**Knowledge** is the seeds about how we know — and how often we don't, and don't know that we don't. Paradigm shifts. The phrase "we used to think." The stories we tell that become more real than the facts they were meant to explain. These seeds are epistemological in the best sense: they make you a more careful, more humble, more delighted inhabitant of your own mind.

A place to start: @link(001) — *The Map That Was Wrong.*

---

That's the map. But remember: you don't have to follow it. The seeds are connected to each other by numbered links — follow any thread that pulls you. The categories will dissolve as you go, and something more like a web will take their place. That's the point.

The garden is large. Start anywhere.

#depth
The seven categories were not derived from a theory. They were induced from the collection — a pragmatic carving of the space that seemed to give readers useful handholds without forcing the ideas into shapes they don't naturally have.

Other carvings were possible. One could organize by scale: quantum → cellular → organismal → social → cosmic. Or by method: empirical → mathematical → phenomenological → speculative. Or by provenance: Whitehead → Gödel → Varela → contemporary AI. Each would illuminate different connections and obscure others.

The choice of seven is not magical, though seven is a satisfying number for a human working memory. The choice of these seven reflects what the collection has actually grown toward: a sustained interest in consciousness and its physical underpinnings (consciousness, sensation, physics), a fascination with the structural patterns that generate complexity from simplicity (mathematics, biology), and an urgent engagement with the new entities and the old epistemological questions they reactivate (AI-minds, knowledge).

What the categories share, underneath their differences, is a single recurring move: finding that the familiar concept — matter, sensation, number, life, mind, knowledge — is stranger and more interesting than the standard picture allows. Each category is a department of the same renovation project.

The reader who follows connections rather than categories will eventually notice that the seeds form clusters regardless of their labels: a cluster around the event-not-thing ontology (@link(002), @link(007), @link(008)); a cluster around emergence and complexity (@link(009), @link(011), @link(012), @link(034)); a cluster around the limits of output as evidence (@link(006), @link(021), @link(022), @link(025)). These cross-category clusters are where the project's deeper structure lives.

#term category: As used in this archive: one of seven provisional groupings used to give readers initial orientation. Not intended as a formal taxonomy. Seeds often belong to more than one and are assigned by primary hook.

#script
OPEN ON: An aerial view of a large, wild garden. No obvious paths. Dense. Beautiful. Slightly overwhelming.

A gate. A visitor stands at it, looking in.

NARRATOR (V.O.): Every garden needs some paths.

CUT TO: The visitor stepping in. Following a stone path through a section of the garden — tall plants, unfamiliar shapes.

NARRATOR (V.O.): Not because nature organizes itself neatly. It doesn't.

CUT TO: A hand placing a small colored flag in the earth. Then another. Then another, a different color. The garden begins to have rough sections — not walls, just flags.

NARRATOR (V.O.): But a visitor at the gate needs somewhere to begin.

RAPID SEQUENCE — seven brief images, one per category, each barely a second:

— A close-up eye, looking inward. (CONSCIOUSNESS)
— A tuning fork, vibrating. (SENSATION)
— A field of stars, then quantum foam. (PHYSICS)
— The Mandelbrot boundary. (MATHEMATICS)
— A cell dividing under a microscope. (BIOLOGY)
— A terminal, a cursor blinking. (AI-MINDS)
— An old map, wrong. (KNOWLEDGE)

NARRATOR (V.O.): Seven sections. Somewhat arbitrarily drawn.

HOLD ON: The garden from above again. The colored flags are visible — faint, useful, not insistent.

NARRATOR (V.O.): The categories will dissolve as you go. Something more like a web will take their place.

CUT TO: The visitor, deeper in now, no longer following the path — just moving toward whatever is interesting.

NARRATOR (V.O.): That's the point.

FINAL IMAGE: The garden, wide. The visitor is small in it, moving. The flags are barely visible from this distance.

FADE.
