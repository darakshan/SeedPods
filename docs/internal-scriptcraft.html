ScriptCraft

These notes are intended for addition to the Structure page under Layer 4: Script. Learned in practice, sessions of 2026-03-11 and 2026-03-22.

The script is not an illustrated voiceover. It is a film. Start from what the camera can show and earn every word of narration.

On format Write the script as a numbered list of scenes, not as flowing prose with CUT TO / VOICEOVER headers scattered through it. Each numbered entry is one scene: the visual direction and any narration together in a single line or two. This is an outline from which a real shooting script will be unfolded later — not the finished script itself. Aim for 4–8 scenes for a typical seedpod. If you are writing more than eight, you are writing a screenplay, not an outline.

On integrating narration Don’t separate VO from its scene. Write it as part of the same numbered entry: the image, then “VO:” and the words. This keeps the visual and the verbal together where they belong, and makes the script readable at a glance.

On music notes If the score matters to the concept, put the composer note as a brief header before scene 1 — not interspersed in the scene list. One short paragraph is enough. Trust the composer with the execution.

On POV The most powerful tool for conveying an interior is point of view. Don’t tell the audience someone is conscious — put the audience inside their experience. They will feel it before they understand it.

On sound Sound design can carry a turn that no narration could. In seedpod #22, the shift from muffled to clear — the moment someone finally addresses Rom directly — is the emotional climax. It costs nothing to write and everything to experience. Sound is the color of film. You don’t always need to go from black and white to color — a shift in acoustic quality can do the same work more subtly.

On time Resist title cards. Let the world of the film show time passing — through objects, through faces, through what has changed in a room. A mother’s hair going from dark to white is worth ten years of title cards. Give the director the idea and trust them with the execution — a brief note in the script is enough.

On voiceover Use it sparingly — only when the image alone genuinely cannot carry the idea. Two moments of voiceover in a three-minute film is probably the maximum. More than that and you are writing an essay, not a film. In the outline format, VO that appears in every scene is a red flag: you are writing narrated slideshow, not a film. If the VO can be cut without losing the scene’s meaning, cut it.

On the closing image The script should end on something the eye can hold, not something the ear just heard. Text on black is a last resort. A held image is better. The final frame should be able to stand alone as a still.

On props and objects Objects do narrative work that dialogue cannot. A monitor growing more sophisticated over the years, a phone becoming a smartphone, a mother’s reading glasses appearing and then thickening — these are not decoration. They are the story told sideways, which is often the most powerful way to tell it.

On compression The original scene outline for a three-minute seedpod should fit comfortably on one screen. If it doesn’t, it hasn’t been edited. Everything that belongs in a shooting script — exact dialogue, camera angles, performance notes, scene-by-scene music cues — belongs in a later document, not in the #script layer. The #script layer answers: what do we see, in what order, and what if anything do we hear? Nothing more.

General principle Show the inside by controlling what the audience sees and hears from the outside. The script’s job is to engineer that experience — not to explain it.
